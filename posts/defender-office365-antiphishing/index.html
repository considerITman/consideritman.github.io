Defender for Office 365 Anti-Phishing: Stop Impersonation and AiTM Attacks

Configure Defender for Office 365 anti-phishing policies to block impersonation attacks, spoof intelligence, and AiTM session cookie theft in Microsoft 365.
May 23, 2026 · 11 min · Alexander Schirmer

Identity-based attacks rose 32% in H1 2025, with AiTM phishing and impersonation campaigns driving most of that growth (Microsoft Digital Defense Report, 2025). Microsoft Defender for Office 365 anti-phishing policies are the primary defence against these attacks, specifically stopping the email delivery that makes impersonation and AiTM campaigns possible. If your policies aren’t explicitly configured, you’re relying on defaults that were never designed to stop modern phishing kits.

Key Takeaways

  • Anti-phishing impersonation protection requires MDO Plan 1 or higher; it is not included in EOP (Exchange Online Protection) alone
  • Spoof intelligence is on by default; impersonation protection for specific users and domains must be explicitly enabled
  • AiTM phishing bypasses MFA by stealing the session cookie after authentication succeeds, not by stealing the password
  • Standard and Strict preset security policies are the recommended starting point; custom policies are for exceptions
  • E3 includes EOP only; E5 or the MDO add-on is required for impersonation protection features (Microsoft, 2025)

Why Does AiTM Phishing Bypass MFA?#

Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) phishing doesn’t steal passwords. It sits as a reverse proxy between the user and a legitimate authentication service, relaying the authentication in real time. The user completes MFA successfully, the attacker captures the resulting session cookie, and then replays that cookie from a different device. MFA was never asked for again. The attack didn’t break MFA; it moved past it entirely.

The Microsoft Digital Defense Report 2025 names AiTM and device code phishing as the two primary techniques behind the 32% rise in identity attacks in H1 2025. Both techniques specifically target the post-authentication token, not the credential itself. Standard anti-phishing policies that focus on link rewriting and malware scanning don’t stop these attacks at the delivery stage. Only policies targeting the phishing infrastructure do.

This is exactly why MDO anti-phishing configuration matters. Blocking the phishing email before the user clicks is the only reliable control point you have. Once the session cookie is captured, detection and response become your only options. Prevention is cheaper.

MFA still blocks over 99% of automated credential attacks (Microsoft Digital Defense Report, 2025). AiTM attacks are sophisticated, targeted campaigns, not mass spray-and-pray. But 60% of breaches still involve a human element (Verizon DBIR, 2025), and credential abuse accounts for 22% of initial access vectors in confirmed breaches (Verizon DBIR, 2025). The combination means targeted phishing remains high-value.

What License Do You Need for Anti-Phishing Protection?#

Impersonation protection is not included in Exchange Online Protection, which is what E3 provides by default. This surprises many administrators who assume all anti-phishing features come with the base Microsoft 365 subscription. The table below shows exactly what you get at each tier.

MDO Plan 1 is included in Microsoft 365 Business Premium and available as a standalone add-on for E3 users. MDO Plan 2 is included in Microsoft 365 E5 and Microsoft 365 E5 Security. If you’re on E3 without the MDO add-on, you have spoof intelligence and basic anti-phishing, but impersonation protection for specific users and domains is not available (Microsoft Service Descriptions, 2025).

Should You Start with Preset Security Policies?#

Yes. Microsoft’s preset security policies, Standard and Strict, are the right starting point for nearly every organisation. They include anti-phishing protection at tested thresholds and are updated automatically by Microsoft as the threat landscape changes. Building a custom policy from scratch means taking on the maintenance burden of keeping thresholds current.

Standard protection is the baseline for all users. Strict protection adds higher aggressiveness and is designed for priority accounts: executives, finance staff, IT administrators, and anyone whose inbox is a high-value target. The recommended approach is to apply the Standard preset to all users first, then layer the Strict preset on top of priority account groups.

Policy priority order determines which policy wins when multiple rules match the same recipient. Strict takes precedence over Standard, custom policies apply by number (lower number = higher priority), and the default policy applies last (Microsoft Learn, 2025).

Configure preset policies at: https://security.microsoft.com/presetSecurityPolicies

How Do You Configure Anti-Phishing Impersonation Protection?#

Impersonation protection requires explicit configuration. No users or domains are protected by default, even with MDO in place. You must add the specific accounts and domains you want protected, then set the actions to take when impersonation is detected.

Navigate to: https://security.microsoft.com/antiphishing

Create a new policy or edit an existing custom policy. Work through the impersonation settings in this order.

User Impersonation Protection#

Add up to 350 specific users whose identities you want to protect. These are typically executives, finance staff, IT admins, and legal. The policy monitors inbound email for senders who appear to be impersonating these users based on display name similarity and email domain lookalikes.

Set the action to Quarantine the message for impersonated users. Moving to junk is too lenient for high-value account impersonation.

Domain Impersonation Protection#

Add your own accepted domains and any partner domains you regularly receive email from. MDO will flag inbound messages from domains that closely resemble your protected domains: typosquats, homoglyph substitutions, and lookalike domains.

You can add up to 1,024 entries across the trusted senders and domains list. Include your primary domain and any secondary or legacy domains your organisation uses.

Mailbox Intelligence and Intelligence-Based Impersonation Protection#

Mailbox intelligence is on by default in all anti-phishing policies. It learns normal communication patterns for each mailbox. The intelligence-based impersonation protection toggle is separate and must be enabled explicitly. It uses that learned communication graph to identify messages from senders the user has never corresponded with but whose display names match protected users (Microsoft Learn, 2025).

First-Contact Safety Tips#

Enable first-contact safety tips. When a recipient receives an email from a sender for the first time, a yellow banner appears in the message notifying them. This is a low-friction user awareness control that doesn’t require any training programme.

The first-contact safety tip is one of the most underused controls in MDO. It’s entirely passive from the user’s perspective but measurably increases the time users spend evaluating unfamiliar senders. Combined with user impersonation protection, it creates a second signal path: the policy acts on the back end, the safety tip acts on the front end.

How Does Spoof Intelligence Work?#

Spoof intelligence is on by default in every anti-phishing policy, including those in the base EOP tier available with E3. It monitors all domains that send email on behalf of your organisation and automatically classifies senders as allowed or blocked based on email authentication results (SPF, DKIM, DMARC).

Two visual indicators appear in Outlook and Outlook on the Web when spoof intelligence detects issues. A question mark on the sender photo indicates the message failed SPF or DKIM authentication and also failed DMARC. A “via” tag appears when the DKIM signing domain or the MAIL FROM domain differs from the From address. Both indicators are visible to end users without any configuration.

Manage allow and block entries for spoofed senders at: https://security.microsoft.com/spoofintelligence

Override entries in the Tenant Allow/Block List under the Spoofed senders tab never expire. Review this list periodically to remove stale overrides, particularly for vendors or partners whose email infrastructure has changed.

One important note: do not disable anti-spoofing if your MX record doesn’t point directly to Microsoft 365. If you route inbound mail through a third-party gateway or on-premises server before it reaches Exchange Online, the inbound IP will always fail SPF checks and anti-spoofing will block legitimate mail. Use Enhanced Filtering for Connectors instead. This configuration tells MDO to evaluate the original sending IP, not the gateway IP.

Spoof intelligence in Microsoft Defender for Office 365 automatically evaluates all inbound messages against SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records, classifying senders as allowed or blocked without administrator intervention. It is included in Exchange Online Protection at no additional cost and is active by default in all anti-phishing policies. Override entries for spoofed senders are managed in the Tenant Allow/Block List and do not expire automatically. (Microsoft Learn, 2025)

PowerShell: Automate Anti-Phishing Policy Configuration#

The Microsoft 365 Defender portal gives you full GUI coverage for anti-phishing policies. PowerShell is the right choice when you need to deploy consistent policy configurations across multiple tenants or want to bring policy state under version control.

The script below uses the ExchangeOnlineManagement v3 module with parameter splatting for readability. It creates a named anti-phishing policy with all impersonation and spoof intelligence controls enabled, then creates a rule that applies the policy to a specific recipient domain.

#Requires -Modules ExchangeOnlineManagement

Connect-ExchangeOnline -ShowBanner:$false

$policyParams = @{
    Name                                = 'Impersonation Protection Policy'
    EnableMailboxIntelligence           = $true
    EnableMailboxIntelligenceProtection = $true
    EnableOrganizationDomainsProtection = $true
    EnableSimilarUsersSafetyTips        = $true
    EnableSimilarDomainsSafetyTips      = $true
    EnableUnusualCharactersSafetyTips   = $true
    MailboxIntelligenceProtectionAction = 'Quarantine'
    EnableSpoofIntelligence             = $true
    AuthenticationFailAction            = 'MoveToJmf'
    EnableFirstContactSafetyTips        = $true
    ErrorAction                         = 'Stop'
}
New-AntiPhishPolicy @policyParams

$ruleParams = @{
    Name              = 'Impersonation Protection Rule'
    AntiPhishPolicy   = 'Impersonation Protection Policy'
    RecipientDomainIs = 'contoso.com'
    Priority          = 0
    ErrorAction       = 'Stop'
}
New-AntiPhishRule @ruleParams

The EnableOrganizationDomainsProtection parameter enables domain impersonation protection for all accepted domains in the tenant automatically. You don’t need to enumerate them individually.

To protect specific users from impersonation, add the TargetedUsersToProtect parameter to New-AntiPhishPolicy. The parameter accepts an array of strings in the format "Display Name;email@domain.com". Add one entry per protected user, up to 350 total.

To audit your existing policy configuration, use Get-AntiPhishPolicy | Select-Object Name, EnableMailboxIntelligenceProtection, EnableOrganizationDomainsProtection, EnableSpoofIntelligence to verify which controls are active across all policies in the tenant.

How Do You Monitor Anti-Phishing Detections?#

Monitoring is split across two views depending on your MDO licence tier. Both are available from the Microsoft Defender portal.

For all MDO customers, the anti-phishing insights page at https://security.microsoft.com/antiphishing provides a summary of detections by policy and by detection type. The Insights tab shows trends over the selected time period.

The spoof intelligence insight at https://security.microsoft.com/spoofintelligence shows the full list of domains that have been auto-classified, along with the infrastructure each sender uses. Review this weekly when first deploying to catch legitimate senders that have been incorrectly blocked.

MDO Plan 2 users can access Threat Explorer at https://security.microsoft.com/threatexplorer. The Phish view in Threat Explorer shows full campaign correlation data, including which users were targeted by the same phishing kit, the sending infrastructure, and the URLs used. This is where investigation starts when a user reports a suspicious message.

MDO Plan 1 users have Real-time Detections, which provides the same Phish view without campaign correlation. You can pivot from a single message to see other messages from the same sender, but the automated campaign grouping is an MDO Plan 2 feature (Microsoft Learn, 2025).

In practice, the spoof intelligence insight page generates the most actionable findings in the first two weeks after deployment. Third-party senders that use shared sending infrastructure on behalf of your domain are frequently classified as blocked. Check the page daily for the first fortnight and build up your allow list before end users notice delivery failures.

Frequently Asked Questions#

What is the difference between anti-phishing in EOP and Defender for Office 365?#

EOP anti-phishing covers spoof intelligence and basic header-based detection. MDO adds user impersonation protection, domain impersonation protection, and mailbox intelligence-based detection. These additional controls require MDO Plan 1 or higher, which is included in M365 Business Premium and M365 E5, or available as an add-on for E3. (Microsoft Service Descriptions, 2025)

Does anti-phishing protection stop AiTM phishing?#

MDO anti-phishing targets the delivery mechanism, blocking the phishing email before the user can click. It doesn’t operate after authentication. AiTM attacks that successfully deliver their lure to the inbox and receive a click still succeed post-delivery. Layering Conditional Access sign-in frequency policies and token protection alongside MDO anti-phishing is the more complete control combination.

What does spoof intelligence do in Microsoft 365?#

Spoof intelligence monitors all inbound messages and evaluates whether the sending domain passes SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authentication. It automatically classifies senders as allowed or blocked and surfaces the results in the Tenant Allow/Block List. It is included in EOP at no additional cost and is on by default in all anti-phishing policies.

How many users can I protect with user impersonation protection?#

Each anti-phishing policy supports up to 350 protected users. You can create multiple custom policies with different user sets and apply them to different recipient groups to work around this limit. In practice, 350 covers most organisations’ executive, finance, and IT admin populations without needing multiple policies.

Do I need MDO Plan 2 for anti-phishing policies?#

No. Anti-phishing policies with full impersonation protection are available in MDO Plan 1. MDO Plan 2 adds Threat Explorer for advanced campaign investigation, Attack Simulation Training, and Advanced Hunting. For policy configuration and basic monitoring, Plan 1 is sufficient. E3 includes EOP but not MDO; E5 includes MDO Plan 2.
