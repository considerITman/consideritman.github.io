How to Configure DLP Policies in Microsoft 365

Configure DLP policies in Microsoft Purview step by step. Covers SITs, simulation mode, Teams, Endpoint, and Copilot. Data breaches cost $4.44M in 2025.
May 22, 2026 · 14 min · Alexander Schirmer

Data breaches cost organizations a global average of $4.44 million in 2025, according to the IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report. For U.S.-based companies that number jumps to $10.22 million. Organizations that deployed AI and automation in their security workflows saved an average of $1.9 million per breach in the same report. Microsoft 365 Data Loss Prevention (DLP) is one of the most direct controls you can place between your sensitive data and an incident. This guide walks through every step: portal navigation, policy anatomy, scoping, sensitive information types, simulation mode, tuning, and monitoring.

Key Takeaways

  • Data breaches cost a global average of $4.44M in 2025 (IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025).
  • DLP policies are configured exclusively in the Microsoft Purview portal (purview.microsoft.com).
  • Always start in simulation mode before enforcing; phased rollouts in 3 steps cut user disruption.
  • Teams, Endpoint, and Copilot DLP all require E5 or the E5 Compliance add-on.
  • Shadow AI added $670,000 to average breach costs in 2025, making Copilot-scoped DLP urgent.

What Is DLP in Microsoft 365 and Why Does It Matter in 2025?#

DLP policies detect and block the accidental or intentional sharing of sensitive data across email, SharePoint, Teams, endpoints, and Copilot. Configure them correctly and you have a technical control that covers both external breach vectors and the negligent insider — who caused 55% of 2025 insider incidents (Ponemon Institute / DTEX 2025 Cost of Insider Risks Report, 2025).

Insider threats are a separate, compounding risk. The average annualized cost of an insider incident hit $17.4 million in 2025, with 55% of incidents caused by negligent employees rather than malicious actors (Ponemon Institute / DTEX 2025 Cost of Insider Risks Report, 2025). Your DLP policy is often the only technical control that catches a careless employee attaching a client spreadsheet to a personal email.

The scale of the external threat is just as stark. Verizon’s 2025 DBIR recorded 12,195 confirmed data breaches, the highest single-year count ever, with ransomware appearing in 44% of all breaches and third-party involvement doubling to 30% (Verizon 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report, 2025). DLP covers the internal and outbound vectors that perimeter controls miss.

The shadow AI problem sharpens the urgency further. IBM’s 2025 report found that shadow AI usage added $670,000 to average breach costs, and 1 in 5 breaches involved shadow AI. With Microsoft 365 Copilot now generally available, you need DLP scoped to Copilot or you have a blind spot.

Where Does DLP Live: Navigating the Microsoft Purview Compliance Portal?#

DLP policies are configured at purview.microsoft.com. Once inside, the path is: Data loss prevention in the left navigation, then Policies, then Create policy. The old Microsoft 365 Compliance Center (compliance.microsoft.com) now redirects to Purview; it is no longer the active admin surface.

If you cannot see the DLP menu, check your role assignment. You need the DLP Compliance Management or Compliance Administrator role in Microsoft Entra ID. The Security Reader role gives read-only access to existing policies but blocks creation.

DLP Policy Anatomy: Rules, Conditions, Actions, and Notifications#

A DLP policy has three tiers: the Policy sets scope (which workloads and users are targeted), the Rule contains matching logic (a single policy can have multiple rules at different severity thresholds), and Conditions plus Actions are the detection and response mechanisms inside each rule. Understanding this hierarchy before clicking “Create policy” prevents structural mistakes that require rebuilding from scratch.

The Policy layer defines scope: which workloads (Exchange, SharePoint, Teams, etc.) and which users or groups the policy targets. The Rule layer contains the logic. A single policy can hold multiple rules with different severity thresholds, so you can audit low-count matches and block high-count matches from the same policy object.

Conditions specify what DLP looks for: sensitive information types (SITs), sensitivity labels, keywords, sender domain, recipient domain, or a combination. Actions specify what happens when conditions match.

ActionEnforcement LevelUser Impact

BlockHard blockAction is stopped; no override available

User notifications and policy tips are the in-app messages end users see in Outlook, Teams, or SharePoint when a rule matches. A well-written policy tip explains what triggered the warning and what the user should do, which reduces help desk tickets significantly.

Should You Use Built-In Templates or Build Custom DLP Policies?#

Built-in templates are the fastest starting point, but they nearly always need tuning before you enforce them. Microsoft ships templates for more than a dozen regulatory and industry frameworks, grouped into categories including Financial data (PCI DSS, credit card numbers), Medical and health (HIPAA), and Privacy (GDPR, U.S. state privacy laws, and others).

Use a custom policy when your organization has proprietary data types, internal project codes, or when a built-in template generates too many false positives out of the box. Custom policies let you define your own SITs with regex patterns and keyword lists.

One common trap: default match thresholds for some SITs are set to “any 1 instance.” A credit card SIT configured with a threshold of 1 will fire on a test email with a single dummy number. Always review and raise the confidence level and instance count before moving out of simulation mode.

Sensitive Information Types: Built-In, Custom, and Exact Data Match#

A sensitive information type (SIT) is the detection engine inside a DLP condition: it defines the pattern to match, supporting keywords, and a confidence level — and the confidence level you choose determines whether your policy catches real threats or generates alert floods on day one. Microsoft ships over 350 built-in SITs covering credit card numbers, Social Security numbers, passport numbers, and medical record numbers.

Custom SITs let you define proprietary patterns. If your organization uses a project code format like PRJ-XXXX-YYYY, you can build a custom SIT with that regex and tie a DLP rule to it.

Exact Data Match (EDM) goes further. It fingerprints a structured dataset, such as an employee ID database or customer list, so DLP can detect exact values from that dataset with near-zero false positives. EDM requires E5 Compliance.

Confidence levels matter in practice. High confidence matches are more specific and generate fewer false positives. Medium and Low confidence matches cast a wider net and are better suited for audit-only rules where you want visibility without user disruption.

How Do You Deploy DLP Safely Using Simulation Mode?#

Always start in simulation mode. Organizations that skip it and go straight to enforcement typically spend the first week managing override requests rather than doing security work. Simulation runs full policy evaluation across all configured locations without enforcing any action, generating matched-item data you can inspect before users are affected. Simulation mode replaced the old “Test” mode as of the November 2025 Microsoft Purview update (Microsoft Learn, retrieved 2026-05-22).

The simulation dashboard has three tabs: Matched items (files and messages that triggered the policy), Alerts (rule-level alerts generated during simulation), and Scan status (which locations have been evaluated). Simulations run for up to 15 days; results are retained for 30 days.

The recommended phased rollout has three steps:

DLP Phased Deployment Approach
PhaseStateScopeUser Impact
1SimulationAll locationsNone
2Simulation + Policy TipsPilot group (50 users)Notifications only
3Full EnforcementAll target usersBlocked or audited

Use PowerShell to check the current mode and priority of all policies in your tenant:

# Connect to Security & Compliance PowerShell (ExchangeOnlineManagement v3)
Connect-IPPSSession

# List all DLP policies with their current mode and priority
Get-DlpCompliancePolicy | Select-Object Name, Mode, Priority

Mode will return TestWithNotifications (Phase 2), TestWithoutNotifications (Phase 1), or Enable (Phase 3). Confirm the correct phase before promoting to enforcement.

How Do You Tune DLP Policies to Reduce False Positives?#

False positives are the primary reason DLP policies get disabled — and the most common trigger is a broad SIT with an instance count of 1. One alert flood during a product launch and the policy is off by end of day. The tuning workflow below works from simulation data rather than gut feel, which is the only reliable way to predict match volume before enforcement begins.

  1. Open Activity Explorer in the Purview portal and filter by your policy name during the simulation window.
  2. Identify which SIT is generating the most unexpected matches.
  3. Raise the confidence level from Medium to High for that SIT in the rule condition.
  4. Raise the instance count. For broad SITs like Credit Card Number, require 5 or more instances rather than 1.
  5. Add exceptions: exclude specific sender domains, SharePoint site URLs, or security groups that generate legitimate matches.
  6. Re-run simulation after each change and review the new matched-item count before enforcing.

The Block with Override action is useful during early enforcement. Users who receive a block can enter a business justification and proceed anyway. You capture the justification in Activity Explorer and can identify legitimate workflows that need exceptions before tightening the policy further.

How Do You Monitor DLP Activity with Activity Explorer and Defender?#

Monitoring is where DLP earns its place in your security stack. Activity Explorer and DLP Alerts are the two primary tools, and they serve different purposes. Verizon’s 2025 DBIR analyzed 12,195 confirmed data breaches, the highest single-year count on record (Verizon 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report, 2025). Alert fatigue from a misconfigured DLP policy makes your detection posture worse, not better.

Activity Explorer (purview.microsoft.com, then Data classification, then Activity explorer) shows every DLP activity by user, location, SIT, policy, and action. Filter by date range and activity type to build a weekly or monthly review cadence.

DLP Alerts are configured at the rule level. When a rule match occurs in enforcement mode, an alert routes to the DLP Alerts dashboard in the Purview portal. Set alert thresholds carefully: one alert per event for high-severity rules, aggregated alerts for lower-severity ones.

Microsoft Defender XDR surfaces DLP alerts as incidents at security.microsoft.com. This is useful for correlating a DLP match (a user emailed a file containing SSNs) with other Defender signals (that same user logged in from an unusual location 10 minutes earlier). Cross-correlating these signals is where the E5 security stack pays off.

The investment in monitoring infrastructure is well-supported by the data. Organizations now allocate 16.5% of their IT security budget to insider risk management, up from 8.2% in 2023. More tellingly, 65% say their insider risk program pre-empted a data breach before it could cause damage (Ponemon Institute / DTEX 2025 Cost of Insider Risks Report, 2025). A DLP monitoring cadence is a core part of that pre-emption posture.

License Requirements: What You Get at E3, E5, and the Compliance Add-On#

M365 E3 covers DLP for Exchange, SharePoint, and OneDrive — everything else requires E5 or the E5 Compliance add-on. If your rollout scope includes Teams, Endpoint, or Copilot DLP, verify licensing before the kickoff call. A missing E5 entitlement blocks the location from appearing in the scope selector, not the policy creation wizard, which means you can configure a policy that silently covers nothing.

Organizations with mixed license pools (some users on E3, some on E5) can scope E5-only locations to the licensed group using user-scoped policies, so you don’t have to choose between full coverage and compliance.

What Are the Most Common DLP Configuration Mistakes?#

Most DLP failures come from five predictable mistakes. Recognizing them before rollout is faster than recovering from a failed deployment.

Mistake 1: Starting in enforcement mode, not simulation. The first policy a new admin creates should always go into simulation. Jumping straight to enforcement triggers immediate user complaints and a wave of override requests that overwhelm the help desk.

Mistake 2: Leaving instance count at 1 for broad SITs. The Credit Card Number SIT will match any 16-digit number. Set the instance count to 5 or higher for SITs that cover common number patterns.

Mistake 3: Forgetting that Teams requires E5. IT admins regularly discover Teams chat is uncovered after rolling out Exchange and SharePoint DLP. Confirm licensing before the project kickoff, not after.

Mistake 4: No exclusions for internal tools. Automated reports, DevOps pipelines, and HR systems often generate files that match SIT patterns. Broad policies catch these and generate noise. Scope exclusions during Phase 1 simulation.

Mistake 5: No monitoring cadence. A policy set and forgotten drifts. New workflows emerge. SIT patterns evolve. Schedule a monthly Activity Explorer review as a recurring calendar item when you go live.

DLP works best as part of a broader identity and access strategy. If you haven’t reviewed your Conditional Access policies, that is the logical next step — Conditional Access controls who can reach your data; DLP controls what they can do with it once they’re in.

Frequently Asked Questions#

What is the difference between a DLP policy and a DLP rule?#

A DLP policy is the container: it defines which workloads and users are in scope. A DLP rule is the logic inside the policy: it specifies conditions (what to detect) and actions (what to do when a match occurs). One policy can contain multiple rules with different thresholds, so a single policy can both audit low-count and block high-count matches.

Does Microsoft 365 E3 include DLP?#

Yes. M365 E3 includes DLP for Exchange Online, SharePoint Online, and OneDrive for Business. It does not include DLP for Microsoft Teams, Endpoint DLP, or DLP for Microsoft 365 Copilot. Those locations require M365 E5 or the E5 Compliance add-on. Check the Microsoft licensing documentation for your current agreement before scoping a rollout.

Can DLP policies block files in Microsoft Teams?#

Yes, but only with the right license. Teams DLP requires M365 E5 or the E5 Compliance add-on. Once licensed, DLP can detect sensitive content in Teams chat and channel messages, including attachments. Without the correct license, Teams is simply not available as a location in the policy scope selector.

What is simulation mode in Microsoft Purview DLP?#

Simulation mode runs full DLP policy evaluation across all selected locations without enforcing any action. It replaced the legacy “Test” mode as of the November 2025 Purview update. Simulations run for up to 15 days, results are retained for 30 days, and a dedicated dashboard shows matched items, alerts, and scan status. Always use simulation before enabling enforcement.

How long does it take for a new DLP policy to take effect?#

A new DLP policy typically propagates across Exchange Online within 1 hour. SharePoint and OneDrive policies can take up to 24 hours to fully propagate, particularly for content already at rest in large libraries. Endpoint DLP propagation depends on device check-in intervals. Use the Purview portal’s scan status tab in simulation mode to confirm propagation before enforcing (Microsoft Learn, dlp-create-deploy-policy, retrieved 2026-05-22).

Simulate First, Enforce When Ready#

Configuring DLP in Microsoft 365 is not a one-time task. It’s an ongoing practice: build a simulation baseline, tune against real matched data, enforce in phases, and review monthly. The cost of getting it wrong is measurable: $4.44 million average per breach globally, $17.4 million average for insider incidents, and $670,000 in additional exposure if shadow AI is left uncontrolled (IBM, Ponemon / DTEX, 2025).

Start with Exchange and SharePoint (E3 is enough), validate your SITs in simulation, and expand to Teams, Endpoint, and Copilot as your E5 licensing allows. The phased deployment model in this guide gives you the control to do that without disrupting users or triggering a policy rollback.

If you’re building DLP as part of a broader Microsoft Purview compliance program, the next logical step is configuring sensitivity labels and auto-labeling policies, which feed directly into DLP conditions and strengthen detection accuracy without increasing the false-positive rate.
